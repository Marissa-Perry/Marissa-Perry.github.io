About Me

Hello! I am a senior undergraduate student at the University of Texas at Austin studying astronomy and computer science. I am interested in the evolution of galaxies over cosmic time, leveraging large spectroscopic and imaging surveys as well as statistical and machine learning approaches to data analysis.

Research

Over the past two years, I have been actively involved in Prof. Steven Finkelstein's Galaxy Evolution Vertically Integrated Project (GEVIP) undergraduate research group at UT Austin. Below, I showcase my main projects in this group.

High-Redshift Galaxy Catalog Cleaning with ML

I collaborated in developing automated methods for cleaning catalogs of high-redshift galaxies using public JWST NIRCam imaging data. These methods involved (Figure 1) implementing an unsupervised non-linear dimensionality reduction technique with a clustering algorithm and (Figure 2) building a convolutional neural network binary image classifier. These methods were included in Cycle 3 and 4 research proposals for JWST (PI: Prof. Steven Finkelstein).

Figure 1: Dimensionally reduced images of 2,310 high-redshift galaxies from the NGDEEP survey. Each source is represented by their image in the F150W and F277W filters.

Figure 2: Top: CNN architecture. Bottom: example predictions on high-redshift galaxies from the CEERS survey, where predictions near 0 or 1 indicate spurious or real sources, respectively.

Bursty Star Formation in Galaxies at Cosmic Dawn

Over the past few years, James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) observations have challenged our models of galaxy evolution. Studies have found an excessive number of early (z ≳ 10) galaxies at the bright end of the UV luminosity function, which is a measure of the number of galaxies at a given UV luminosity per unit volume of space. Highly stochastic star formation up-scatter galaxies to brighter UV luminosities, potentially explaining this result. The primary goal of this work is to use the star-forming main sequence (SFMS) and the Hα-to-UV ratio to examine the transition from steady to bursty star formation over cosmic time. We analyze 347 galaxies observed with JWST/NIRSpec from the CEERS and RUBIES programs.

Figure 3: Hα- (left) and UV-based (right) SFRs vs. stellar masses for our galaxy sample binned by redshift. The shaded regions denote the 1σ intrinsic scatter intervals about the SFMS. The black dashed and dashed-dotted curves represent literature values from Popesso+2023 and Speagle+2014, respectively.

Figure 4: The fraction of galaxies whose Hα-to-UV ratios are consistent (bottom panel) and inconsistent (top panels) with a constant SFH computed by Mehta+2023 as a function of redshift. These results suggest that higher-mass galaxies evolve from steady to inactive star-formation over cosmic time, and lower-mass galaxies generally experience bursty star-formation, especially at early times.
